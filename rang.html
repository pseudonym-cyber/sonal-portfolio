Product Case Study · 0→1 Concept

Beauty shouldn't start with a search bar.

rang is a personalised beauty discovery & shopping app that starts from who you are — your vibe — not a catalogue of ten thousand products. Because the hard part of beauty was never buying it. It was knowing what's actually for you.

RoleProduct — thesis, strategy, design, prototype
SurfaceMobile app (clickable prototype)
MarketIndia · D2C beauty & personal care
StageConcept → high-fidelity prototype

A generation discovers beauty through reels, hauls and GRWM videos. They don't shop by SKU — they fall for a look, a vibe, a version of themselves they want to try on.

But every beauty app hands them the same thing: a search box on top of an endless shelf. So they save forty reels, screenshot ingredient lists, poll a WhatsApp group — and still aren't sure it's right for them.

rang flips that. Tell us your vibe, and we'll find your rang — the products, the routine and the look that are actually yours. No more buying one more thing that wasn't meant for you.

— why I'm building rang
The belief

Curation is the product. The catalogue is just inventory.

Selection stopped being the moat the day every brand went online. With thousands of near-identical serums and foundations one tap away, more choice doesn't help — it paralyses. What's scarce now is a point of view: someone who looks at all of it and says, this one, for you.

So rang starts from identity, not inventory. Find your rang — chosen for your vibe, not the bestseller list. Every screen is built to make a person feel seen first, then shop what fits with confidence.

But you can't personalise what you don't understand — and you can't fake understanding. Relevance requires honesty. That single line runs through the whole product.

The problem, honestly

The shelf is infinite. The guidance is zero.

Beauty e-commerce solved access and quietly broke discovery. Underneath the pretty grid, two things go wrong for the person trying to choose:

Problem one — choice paralysis

"Which one is even for me?"

Hundreds of look-alike options, ratings averaged over strangers with different skin, and no point of view. So she defaults to the bestseller — or buys nothing at all.

rang turns a wall of SKUs into a short, personal edit built around her vibe — a curated few she can actually trust.

Problem two — the trust gap

"Will this actually work on me?"

Reviews come from people unlike her. She's been burned before by a hyped product that didn't suit her skin, so every new buy feels like a gamble.

rang shows proof from people who share her vibe, plus an AI expert that builds the routine — not just sells the bottle.

The promise underneath both: she tells us her vibe; we do the sifting — and only show her what's genuinely hers.

Who I'm building for first

A 23-year-old who's into it — and overwhelmed by it

The first person rang is for grew up on beauty content. She has a wishlist of looks she's saved and a graveyard of products that didn't suit her. She's not a beginner and not an expert — she's enthusiastic and unsure.

What stops her isn't interest or budget. It's the paralysis of infinite shelves and the fear of wasting money on one more thing that isn't for her. So rang has to make her feel known in the first thirty seconds — and right every time after that.

How rang delivers it

An experience built around being known

Every part of the flow maps to one of the two things in her way — paralysis or doubt. The journey is built around a single shift: from browsing a catalogue to being handed your edit.

Magazine cover→ Pick your world→ Swipe your vibe→ Your rang→ Personalised edit→ People like you→ AI routine→ Shop

Discover by vibe, not keyword

A swipe deck reads your vibe in seconds — no quiz, no search box. Curiosity is the input; a feed that feels like you is the output.

A vibe with a name

"The Glass Skin Girlie" — an identity you recognise, not a faceless "recommended for you." Curation that feels like a friend, not an algorithm.

Proof from people like you

Results and videos from divas who share your vibe — relevance over volume, because beauty outcomes are personal and a 4.5★ average isn't.

An expert that builds the routine

rang. AI is always on to turn products into a routine for your skin — the part every catalogue leaves you to figure out alone.

Product decisions

The choices, and why I made them

At each of these, the obvious e-commerce move would have quietly rebuilt the same overwhelming shelf. So I chose differently.

Removing choice paralysis

Swipe your vibe — never a search bar

The obvious move

A search box and filter rails on top of the full catalogue.

What I did

A playful swipe deck that reads vibe, then opens a short, personal edit.

Why: search only helps people who already know the answer — and most don't. They know the feeling they want, not the SKU. Leading with vibe turns "what do I even look for" into "this is so me," and makes the catalogue feel curated instead of infinite.

Making curation feel personal

A named vibe, not "recommended for you"

The obvious move

A silent "Recommended for you" rail powered by an algorithm.

What I did

A named persona — "The Glass Skin Girlie" — she can see, own and share.

Why: "recommended" is invisible and forgettable; a named identity is memorable, shareable, and makes personalisation feel like being understood rather than tracked. It also gives every screen downstream a single, clear point of view.

Earning trust

"People like you," not aggregate stars

The obvious move

Sort by rating; lead with 4.6★ across 12k reviews.

What I did

Surface real results and videos from people who share her vibe.

Why: a five-star average across strangers with different skin tells her almost nothing. Ten results from people like her tell her everything. Beauty outcomes are personal, so the proof has to be too.

Honesty over false confidence

Admit what we can't rank yet

The obvious move

Rank every product confidently and hide the uncertainty.

What I did

Flag new launches as "not enough data to rank yet, but worth discovering."

Why: faking confidence is exactly how a personalisation product loses trust the first time a "top pick" flops. Saying "we don't know yet, but this is interesting" treats her like an adult — and trust, once earned, is the whole reason she comes back.

The category

Why beauty, why this way

I chose beauty because it's where India's D2C boom is loudest and discovery is most broken — a category with thousands of launches a year, sky-high intent, and no one truly owning personal curation between the brand and the buyer.

  • Two worlds, one identity — skincare and makeup, unified under a single vibe so the edit feels coherent, not bolted together.
  • Vibe-first, not catalogue-first — we lead with who she is and the look she wants, never a grid of SKUs.
  • Repeat by nature — skincare replenishes and routines evolve, so relevance compounds straight into retention.
  • Content is the funnel — beauty is discovered through video; rang makes UGC the proof, not an afterthought.
What's next

If this were funded tomorrow

Phase 1 — Nail the vibe

One vibe engine, two worlds (skin + makeup), curated edits that genuinely feel personal. Win on relevance, not range.

Phase 2 — Own the routine

Ship rang. AI as a true routine-builder with replenishment nudges — so we own usage, not just the first purchase.

Phase 3 — Open the loop

A creator/UGC layer and brand collaborations that turn "people like you" into a living, two-sided engine.

Why I built this

What it shows about how I work

I didn't start rang from a category map or a competitor teardown. I started from a feeling everyone who loves beauty knows — standing in front of infinite choice with no idea what's for you. Then I worked backwards: what would make a person feel known in seconds, trust what she's shown, and shop with confidence — and built the product as the answer to those three.

That's how I think about product: find the precise human moment where someone stalls, and design the specific thing that gets them moving. The thesis, the ICP, the vibe-matching model, the full magazine-to-checkout flow, and a high-fidelity clickable prototype — I took all of it end to end myself, to make the belief real enough to feel, not just describe.
